    Background on Associated Hospital Measures

    Death Among Patients With Treatable Complications This measure is intended to identify patients who pass away following the development of a complication. The underlying assumption is that good hospitals identify these complications quickly and treat them aggressively.
    Sepsis Considered a life-threatening medical emergency, sepsis happens when an infection you already have triggers a chain reaction throughout your body. Without timely treatment, sepsis can rapidly lead to tissue damage, organ failure, and death.
    Clots/Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT) A medical condition that occurs when a blood clot forms in a deep vein (common in the lower leg, thigh, or pelvis). Complications of clots happens when a part of the clot breaks off and travels through the bloodstream to the lungs, preventing blood from getting to your lungs.
    Pressure Sores & Ulcers Caused by unrelieved pressure on the skin. They usually develop over bony prominences, such as the elbow, heel, hip, shoulder, back, and back of the head, creating soft-tissue damage ranging from a cut to deep crater in the skin.
    Wounds Dehiscence Caused by a surgical wound failing to heal. Can also be caused by perforation of a surgical wound where internal organs are exposed.
    Accidental Cuts & Lacerations Looking at the rate of a hospital and physician's inadvertent cuts, punctures, perforations, and lacerations during a surgical procedure is a very good gauge of quality of care.

    For additonal information on measures, see Medicare's Hospital Compare webpage
